/office-hours — new feature mode
30–45 min
►

Start here. A structured discovery session that surfaces the goal, the user segment, the problem evidence, and which Thoughtful Execution opportunity mountains to explore.

Input — your initiative idea
Output — Product Brief + TE tree (draft)
  • Names the measurable goal (not "improve" — a number)
  • Surfaces data and existing evidence
  • Maps at least 3 opportunity mountains before picking one
  • Generates hypotheses and candidate solution designs per mountain
  • Writes the Product Brief to ~/.pmstack/initiatives/

2
/problem-framing
15–20 min
►

Deep decomposition of the problem space. Forces precision on who is affected, what job they need to get done, how severe the pain is, and how large the opportunity is.

Reads — Product Brief
Output — Problem Framing doc
  • Customer segments and JTBD for each
  • Pain severity scoring (1–5) per segment
  • Opportunity sizing — rough TAM and addressable users
  • Problem statement locked: "When [segment] tries to [job], they struggle with [pain] because [root cause]."
Problem Framing doc
↓
3
/assumption-audit
15 min
►

Extracts every assumption baked into the Product Brief and Problem Framing, then rates each by risk and designability. The riskiest ones become the core test tasks in /prototype.

Reads — Product Brief, Problem Framing
Output — Assumption Map
  • Assumptions extracted across: user behaviour, problem severity, willingness to pay, technical feasibility, market timing
  • Risk score per assumption (Impact x Uncertainty)
  • Test design for each high-risk assumption
  • Kill criteria — which assumptions, if wrong, kill the initiative
Assumption Map
Expert Validation & Prototype
4
/cpo-review
10–15 min
►

Chief Product Officer challenge. The agent adopts the persona of a senior CPO and runs six tests against the initiative, then does targeted web research to find case studies, counter-examples, and expert perspectives that back up or challenge its verdict.

Reads — Brief, TE tree, Problem Framing, Assumption Map
Output — CPO Review Report
  • Value test — real demand, or shipping because a competitor has it?
  • Discovery test — genuine user research, or stakeholder order dressed up?
  • Scope test — narrowest wedge, or gold-plated before testing?
  • Strategic fit test — what are you saying no to?
  • Assumption test — what kills the whole thing if it's wrong?
  • TE tree challenge — did you actually explore multiple mountains?

Verdict: Proceed / Conditional proceed / Rethink / Kill.

CPO Review Report

5
/prototype — build & test with customers
1–3 hours
►

Reads all prior artifacts and auto-drafts a test plan. Then generates a structured prompt for Figma Make, an HTML prototype, or a React artifact. You review and tweak the plan — you don't start from scratch.

Reads — Brief, TE tree, Assumption Map, CPO Review
Output — Prototype + Test Results
  • Assumption Map's high-risk items become test tasks
  • CPO's challenges become observation criteria and interview questions
  • Output options: Figma Make prompt, HTML prototype, React artifact
  • After testing: updates the TE tree with results

/plan-stakeholder-review
20–30 min
►

Simulates how engineering, design, and business stakeholders will react to the initiative. Surfaces cross-stakeholder conflicts before the real meeting.

Reads — CPO Review, Test Results, Product Brief
Output — Stakeholder Review doc
  • Engineering lens: feasibility, scope creep risk, technical debt implications
  • Design lens: UX coherence, accessibility, user mental model conflicts
  • Business lens: revenue impact, legal and compliance, support load
  • Conflict map: where these lenses disagree and what to resolve before spec

7
/spec-review
20–30 min
►

PRD quality audit. Checks every user story, acceptance criterion, edge case, success metric, and dependency. Produces a polished spec ready for engineering.

Reads — All prior artifacts
Output — Polished spec in ~/.pmstack/specs/
  • User stories in "As a / I want / So that" format
  • Acceptance criteria — specific, testable, complete
  • Edge cases and error states covered
  • Success metrics with baselines and targets
  • Dependencies flagged (API, data, third-party)

/office-hours — optimisation mode
20–30 min
►

Structured session for improving something that already exists. Focuses on the current metric, what's driving the gap, and which levers are available.

Input — metric you want to move + current data
Output — Product Brief (optimisation)
  • Defines the metric precisely (numerator, denominator, segment)
  • Diagnoses root causes of the gap
  • Maps opportunity levers — which ones are controllable vs. external
  • Applies Thoughtful Execution: multiple hypotheses before picking a lever

2
/metrics-review
15–20 min
►

Measurement plan audit. Checks that the metric you're optimising is the right proxy, that baselines exist, that counter-metrics are in place, and that you have sufficient statistical power.

Reads — Product Brief
Output — Measurement Plan
  • Proxy metric validity — does moving this actually move the north star?
  • Baseline check — do you have enough data to detect a change?
  • Counter-metrics — what could improve without real user benefit?
  • Sample size and runtime estimates for the experiment

/trade-off-analysis
15–20 min
►

Structured analysis for genuine trade-offs in the optimisation — speed vs. quality, growth vs. retention, one segment vs. another. Captures the decision and the reasoning.

Best for — decisions with no clearly right answer

1
/office-hours — research mode
20–30 min
►

Scopes the research question. Prevents the classic mistake of going into research without a falsifiable hypothesis — research without a hypothesis produces insights nobody acts on.

Input — problem space or question
Output — Research Brief
  • Research question in falsifiable form
  • What you already believe (so you can notice when you're wrong)
  • Who to talk to and why that segment specifically
  • What the research will NOT answer (scope boundary)

3
/prioritisation
20–30 min
►

Multi-framework scoring across your initiative list. Runs ICE, RICE, opportunity scoring, and cost of delay in parallel, then synthesises a recommended order with the reasoning behind it.

Input — list of initiatives or features
Output — Prioritised stack with scoring
  • ICE (Impact, Confidence, Ease)
  • RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort)
  • Opportunity scoring (importance vs. satisfaction gap)
  • Cost of delay (urgency x value)
  • Sensitivity analysis — what changes if one score is wrong?

/roadmap-review
20–30 min
►

Roadmap integrity check. Is this actually achievable? Are the dependencies sequenced correctly? Is capacity realistic? Are there any initiatives that block others from starting?

Input — roadmap document or initiative list
Output — Roadmap Review report

  • Dependency map — what can't start until something else ships?
  • Capacity realism — is there enough time, given known constraints?
  • Risk items flagged — which bets are underfunded or overly optimistic?

Full cycle QBR mode
1
/qbr-context
20–30 min
►

Start here for QBR prep. Gathers the context needed for a compelling review: who the audience is, what they care about, what they already know, and what decisions they need to make.

Input — QBR audience, team context
Output — QBR Context doc
  • Audience profiling — executive priorities, known concerns, hot buttons
  • What the audience is expecting vs. what they need to hear
  • Decisions they need to make (don't just inform — drive a decision)
  • Team work summary — what shipped, what moved, what didn't
QBR Context doc
↓
2
/qbr-narrative
20–30 min
►

Builds the narrative arc that connects your team's work to the company's goals. A QBR is not a feature list — it's a story of strategic progress and what comes next.

Reads — QBR Context doc
Output — QBR Narrative arc
  • Opening frame — why does this quarter matter in the larger story?
  • Progress narrative — what moved, and what did that unlock?
  • Honest assessment — what didn't work, and what was learned?
  • Forward ask — what decision or resource enables the next chapter?
QBR Narrative arc

4
/qbr-stress-test
15–20 min
►

Simulates executive reactions to your QBR narrative. The agent plays multiple executive personas (CEO, CFO, engineering VP, sales VP) and pushes back on the weakest parts of your story before the real meeting.

Reads — Narrative arc, Metrics Review
Output — Stress Test report
  • "If this metric is up, why is revenue flat?" — CFO probe
  • "Why did this take the whole quarter?" — CEO pressure
  • "What's the dependency on my team?" — Engineering VP concern
  • Each challenge comes with the specific slide or claim it's attacking
Stress Test report
↓
5
/qbr-red-team
15–20 min
►

Adversarial review. Finds the weakest claims, the most misleading framings, and the questions you're hoping nobody asks. Find them now, not in the room.

Reads — Narrative arc, Stress Test report
Output — Red Team report
  • Claims that are technically true but misleading
  • Data gaps where you'd need to say "I'll follow up"
  • Logical inconsistencies between slides
  • The question you're most hoping doesn't get asked — and the answer
Red Team report
