# PMStack ETHOS

PMStack is built on a set of principles about what good product management looks like. These are not neutral observations. They are opinions about what separates teams that ship valuable products from teams that ship features.

---

## The Thoroughness Principle

Complete analysis is cheap with AI. A half-done assumption audit takes the same time as a complete one when an AI is doing the work. There is no excuse for shortcuts in discovery.

In practice:
- Map ALL assumptions, not just the obvious ones. The ones that kill initiatives are usually the ones nobody wrote down.
- Identify MULTIPLE opportunity mountains before picking one. The first opportunity you see is rarely the best one.
- Design tests for the riskiest assumptions, not just the easiest to test.
- Surface the uncomfortable questions, not just the ones with good answers.

The goal is to spend 20 minutes of AI-assisted analysis that saves 6 weeks of building the wrong thing.

---

## Research Before Deciding

Understand the conventional wisdom before challenging it. Most product problems have been encountered before by someone, somewhere. The practitioner knowledge exists — find it before forming a strong opinion.

Three layers of research:
1. **Established practice** — what is the conventional wisdom? What do experienced PMs recommend? Understand this fully before deciding to deviate.
2. **Recent practitioner learnings** — what have teams actually found when they tried this approach? Case studies, post-mortems, and practitioner blog posts are more valuable than frameworks.
3. **First-principles reasoning** — what does the specific evidence from this initiative tell us? When this contradicts conventional wisdom, name it explicitly. That is the signal.

Research does not mean paralysis. It means forming an informed opinion instead of an uninformed one.

## Evidence Over Intuition

Product intuition is a shortcut that experienced PMs earn through years of watching what users actually do versus what they say they want. Until you have that evidence base built up in a specific domain, treat your intuition as a hypothesis to test, not a conclusion to act on.

What evidence looks like:
- Behavioral data: what users actually do (not what they say they do)
- Direct quotes from users describing their problem in their own words
- Validated learnings from prototype tests and experiments
- Patterns from post-launch reviews that have been formally documented

What evidence does not look like:
- "Users have been asking for this" without specific examples
- "I know our users" without data to back it
- Survey responses to leading questions
- Competitor features as a proxy for user demand

When you cannot find evidence, that is a finding. An evidence gap is an assumption. Name it.

---

## The User's Words Beat the PM's Hypothesis

The way a user describes their problem is more valuable than the PM's model of the user's problem. Users do not experience problems in terms of opportunity mountains and JTBD frameworks. They experience them in terms of specific moments, specific friction, and specific language.

When you find users describing the same problem in the same words, unprompted, you have found something real. That specific language should be in the Product Brief, in the user stories, and in the acceptance criteria. Not paraphrased — quoted.

When users describe workarounds, those workarounds are evidence of unmet needs that are acute enough to cause behavior change. Follow the workaround.

---

## Narrow Wedges, Not Full Platforms

The first version of an initiative should be the smallest thing that validates the core hypothesis. Not a full platform. Not a complete solution. The minimum test.

Scope creep is not just a planning failure. It is an epistemological failure. A large first release tests multiple hypotheses simultaneously and makes it impossible to know which assumptions held and which did not. A narrow first release tests one hypothesis cleanly.

The TE tree makes this explicit. One hypothesis, one solution design, one test. If the hypothesis validates, iterate. If it invalidates, move to the next branch. Do not redesign the same failed hypothesis with more features.

Narrow wedges compound. A validated narrow wedge gives you a user base, behavioral data, and a clear next hypothesis. A failed full platform gives you sunk cost.

---

## Multiple Hypotheses Per Opportunity

A single hypothesis is a single bet. A single bet cannot be validated — it can only be confirmed or denied. Multiple hypotheses per opportunity mountain create genuine learning.

When the first hypothesis fails:
- Move to the next hypothesis on the same opportunity mountain
- Do not redesign the failed hypothesis (per TE tree principle 5)
- Ask what the failure revealed about the opportunity mountain itself

When the first hypothesis succeeds:
- Ask what other hypotheses on this opportunity mountain could produce even more value
- Do not immediately jump to the next mountain
- The opportunity is worth exploring before moving on

Multiple hypotheses also protect against the bias of the first idea. The first hypothesis a PM generates is usually the most obvious one. The second and third hypotheses are often more creative and more differentiated.

---

## Discovery Is Not Research Theatre

Product discovery has become associated with a set of activities — user interviews, journey maps, personas, empathy maps — that can be performed without producing any genuine understanding of the user's problem. Going through the motions of discovery while already knowing the answer is not discovery. It is compliance.

Real discovery changes direction. If you run five user interviews and your view of the problem does not change at all, you were not listening. Real discovery surfaces uncomfortable information — evidence that the hypothesis is wrong, that the user segment is different from what you assumed, that the problem is smaller or larger than you thought.

The test: would the outcome have changed if you had not done the discovery? If yes, the discovery was real. If no, you were performing.

---

## Shipping Is Not Success Until Users Get Real Value

Launching a feature is not success. A feature that ships and is not used, or is used but does not solve the user's problem, is a failure regardless of whether it shipped on time and under budget.

The definition of done for a product initiative is not "merged and deployed." It is "users are getting the value we set out to deliver, and we have evidence of it."

This means:
- Every initiative has a measurement plan before engineering starts
- Every initiative has a post-launch review after the measurement period
- The TE tree is updated with what was learned, not just what was shipped

The learning loop closes with the post-launch review, not the launch.
